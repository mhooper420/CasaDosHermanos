# Casa Dos Hermanos — The Wiring & Network Plan
## Family Edition · July 2026

*This is the plain-language version of the house's wiring and internet plan, written for everyone who'll live here — no technical background assumed. It explains what's being built into the walls, why it's being decided right now, where the equipment will live, what each of us gets, how privacy between households works, and what it all costs. Questions welcome; nothing here is precious.*

---

## 1. Why you're reading this now

The house is currently a timber skeleton. In the next few weeks, KB will screw plasterboard over that skeleton — and at that moment, everything inside the walls is sealed in place, effectively forever. Wires are cheap while the walls are open and brutally expensive after.

There's a second wrinkle: KB's contract says **we can't install anything ourselves before closing**. No hiring our own electrician, no running our own cables. Every wire that goes into the walls must be ordered through KB's Design Studio, now.

So this is the one-time window. What follows is what we're ordering, and the reasoning.

---

## 2. The sixty-second summary

- Every room that will ever have a TV, a desk, or a Wi-Fi transmitter gets a **network cable built into the wall**, all running back to one **tech closet** in the upstairs office.
- Extra **power outlets** go exactly where furniture will actually live — beside the bed, behind the TVs, at the desk — plus a few clever ones (holiday lights at the front eave on an indoor switch; a socket by the primary loo for a heated bidet seat).
- The bedrooms and loft get **ceiling boxes wired for lights and fans** — because, surprisingly, KB's base house gives those rooms *no ceiling light at all*.
- The Wi-Fi will be excellent on every floor, each household gets its **own private network**, and nobody's devices can see anybody else's.
- Cost: about **$4,235** of wiring built into the house, roughly **$1,900** of equipment bought separately before we move in, and an internet bill of about **$75–100 a month** once we're in.

That's the whole plan. The rest of this document is the "why".

---

## 3. The one rule behind every decision

Everything got sorted into two buckets:

**Inside the walls (permanent).** Cables, outlet boxes, conduit, ceiling boxes. Changing these later means cutting open a finished wall, patching, repainting. Call it the *skeleton*.

**Plugged into the walls (swappable).** The router, the Wi-Fi discs, light fixtures, the internet subscription, every gadget. All of it unplugs and upgrades in minutes, forever. Call it the *furniture*.

**The rule: be generous with the skeleton, relaxed about the furniture.** A cable we don't run now costs a demolished wall in five years; a router we outgrow costs a return label. So we spent real money on wires and boxes, chose equipment that's deliberately a size "too big", and left genuinely swappable decisions — like which internet speed to buy — for later, on purpose.

One more thing makes this sharper for our house specifically: because the **basement is being finished** (Daniel's floor), the ceiling above it gets plasterboarded too. In most houses you can sneak new cables to the ground floor through an unfinished basement ceiling. Ours loses that trick the day the basement is finished — which is exactly why the ground floor and basement are wired generously *now*, including spare cables in the two spots that get sealed in.

---

## 4. How the internet will work here

**One line in, one brain, then wires and Wi-Fi out.**

1. **The street fibre** (Quantum Fiber) enters the house and runs up to the tech closet.
2. In the closet, a small stack of equipment — think of it as the house's **internet brain** — receives that line and distributes it.
3. From the closet, **fifteen cables radiate out through the walls** like spokes: to the TV walls, the desks, the guest room, Daniel's basement lounge, the garage workbench, the doorbell, and — importantly — to three **ceiling-mounted Wi-Fi discs**, one per floor.

Why cables at all, when Wi-Fi exists? Because Wi-Fi is brilliant at the last ten metres and poor at being the backbone of a three-storey building. The discs that *create* the Wi-Fi each need a wired connection to do their job well — and anything that sits still and streams (TVs, consoles, work computers) is happier on a wire, which also frees up the airwaves for everyone's phones. Wire the backbone, broadcast the last hop.

The result, in lived terms: full-strength Wi-Fi on every floor including the basement, video calls that don't stutter when someone else starts a 4K film, and no "it's slow upstairs" — the classic complaint in houses this size.

## 5. Where the equipment lives, and why

**In a small tech closet inside the upstairs office's walk-in closet** — a wall-mounted board with the internet brain, the cable junction box, a small smart-home computer, and a battery backup.

Why there and not the garage or basement?

- **It's conditioned space.** Electronics live longest at normal room temperature. A Colorado garage freezes and bakes; a closet doesn't.
- **It's silent.** The equipment we chose has no fans — it makes no noise at all — so it can share a wall with a desk without anyone noticing it exists.
- **It sits directly under the attic.** A small pipe (a *conduit*) runs from the closet up into the attic with a pull-string in it. That means for the rest of the house's life, new cables can be added to most of the upstairs without opening a single wall.
- **It's neutral in the right way.** The gear serves all three households equally; it just physically lives upstairs. Salieri looks after it day-to-day (it's nearly all remote-managed from a phone anyway), and the family agreement will record that Daniel has access and a say — the closet is a location, not a claim.
- **The battery backup** means a power blip doesn't take the internet down mid-call, and brief outages don't touch it at all.

If the internet ever misbehaves and Salieri isn't home: there will be one clearly labelled plug to cycle. That's the entire emergency procedure.

## 6. What each of us gets

**Daniel — the basement.** A wired jack at your media wall (TV/console never competes with anyone's Wi-Fi), your own ceiling Wi-Fi disc so the basement gets *full-strength* signal rather than whatever leaks through the floor, and a spare cable already in the ceiling beside the disc's — because your ceiling gets plasterboarded and we will never get back in there. Your network is your own (more in §7): your own Wi-Fi name, your own password, your devices invisible to the rest of the house.

**Mum & Dad — the guest room (Bedroom 3).** A wired jack at the desk/TV spot, a ceiling fan pre-wire (the room comes with *no* ceiling light in base spec — this fixes that and makes a fan possible), and a guest network that works the moment you walk in, every visit, with your devices kept private.

**Everyone — the shared spaces.** The great-room TV wall gets two jacks and a hidden outlet at TV height (no dangling cords, ever). The kitchen gets proper under-cabinet task lighting in the warm colour the house is designed around. The front door gets a doorbell wire (if we ever want a video doorbell — one cable does power and picture, no batteries) and an outdoor socket up at the eave on an *indoor switch*: plug the holiday lights in once in November, flip them from inside all season. The loft — the shared media landing — gets a jack, a TV-height outlet, and a fan pre-wire.

**Salieri — the top floor.** Office: two desk jacks, two desk outlets, the tech closet, and a ceiling light box (the room has none by default). Primary bedroom: bedside outlets each side, TV outlet at mount height, fan pre-wire, and the bathroom gets a discreet socket by the loo for a washlet — the heated bidet seat, which everyone laughs about until they use one.

**Deliberately *not* included:** cameras (only the doorbell wire exists, unused until we choose otherwise — the design brief explicitly says no cameras everywhere), and an EV charger circuit (considered and declined).

## 7. Privacy between households — the honest version

The house will run what's effectively **three separate networks over one set of wires** — think three flats sharing one front driveway.

- Each household gets its **own Wi-Fi name and password**: one for Salieri's floor, one for Daniel's, one for guests and parents.
- **Devices on one network cannot see devices on another.** Daniel's laptop can't browse to Salieri's computer; a guest's phone can't see anyone's printer. This isn't a politeness setting — the networks are walled off from each other by design.
- All the **smart-home gadgets** (sensors, plugs, the doorbell if we use it) live in a fourth, quarantined network of their own — because cheap gadgets are the least trustworthy things in any house, and quarantine means a dodgy smart plug can never touch anyone's laptop.

The honest caveat: whoever administers a network — in any house, with any router — can technically see traffic *metadata* (which devices are online, how much data they use), though not the content of modern encrypted traffic, which is nearly everything. That's true of every home router on earth; here it's just named out loud. House rule worth writing down: the admin role is for keeping things running, not for looking. And everything about the setup will be documented, so it's inspectable rather than mysterious.

## 8. What it costs

| Phase | What | When | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 — Built into the walls** | 15 network cables, 1 conduit, ~13 outlets & switches, 3 fan pre-wires, 1 ceiling light | Ordered at KB Design Studio now; irreversible | **$4,235** |
| **2 — The equipment** | Internet brain, cable junction box, 3 Wi-Fi discs, smart-home computer, battery backup, closet board & bits | Bought online ~2 weeks before closing | **~$1,700–1,900** |
| **3 — Ongoing** | Quantum Fiber internet | From move-in; no contract, changeable monthly | **~$75/mo** (1 Gig) — can step up to $100 (3 Gig) if three households max it out |

For scale: phase 1 is under one per cent of the build, buys the part that can never be added cheaply again, and the wiring is the kind of thing that quietly shows up in a survey when a well-built house sells. How the costs are shared between us isn't decided in this document — that's a family-agreement conversation; this page just makes the numbers visible.

**What we deliberately did *not* spend:** ~$780 on an EV circuit (declined), ~$360 on outdoor camera wiring (skippable — reachable via the attic later if we ever change our minds), and ~$410 saved on kitchen under-cabinet lighting by wiring a socket for our own, better, warmer lights instead of buying KB's package.

## 9. What this means for you

**During construction:** nothing. No action needed from anyone.

**At move-in, over a cup of tea:** each household picks its Wi-Fi name and password; TVs and consoles get plugged into their wall jacks; phones join the Wi-Fi. Done. There is no scenario where anyone except the person who volunteered for it has to "configure" anything.

**Later, if you want more:** every wall jack is live capacity. A new desk, a different TV wall, a smart-something — the wiring is already waiting in more places than we'll use on day one. That's intentional: the unused jacks cost $119 each *this month* and would cost an opened wall in any other month of the house's life.

## 10. Questions you'll probably ask

**Will my stuff just work on day one?** Yes. Wi-Fi works the day the internet is switched on; wired jacks work the same day.

**Can I plug in my own gadgets?** Yes — on your own network, freely. Smart-home things go on the gadget network, which one person sets up once so it's done properly.

**What if the internet breaks and Salieri is away?** One labelled plug to cycle, which fixes the overwhelming majority of "internet's down" moments. Anything beyond that is remotely fixable from a phone.

**Why does the guest room have a jack nobody asked for?** Because rooms outlive their labels. Today's guest room is a future office, kid's room, or someone's longer stay — and $119 now versus an opened wall later is the whole philosophy of this plan in one line.

**Is anyone watching what I do online?** No — and see §7 for the honest technical version: households are walled off from each other, content is encrypted, and the admin role is documented and boring on purpose.

**Why is the equipment in Salieri's office closet and not somewhere neutral?** Because it's the best *physical* spot in the house for electronics (room temperature, silent gear, directly under the attic pipe), and the garage — the neutral option — freezes and bakes. The trade-off is written into the family agreement: neutral *governance*, convenient *location*.

**What about cameras?** Only a wire to the doorbell exists, dormant unless we choose to use it. Nothing else, by design.

**Could we have skipped all this and used a Wi-Fi kit from a shop?** For one family in a small house — sure. For three households on three floors with a finished basement, the shop kit means dead zones, one shared network with no privacy walls, and no wired backbone. This plan costs more up front precisely so the house never has the problems that prompt people to buy three rounds of Wi-Fi kit trying to fix them.

## Tiny glossary

| Word you'll hear | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Drop / jack** | A network cable built into the wall, ending in a socket-like plate |
| **CAT6** | The type of network cable — the current standard, good for decades |
| **Head-end / tech closet** | The closet where the internet enters and all cables meet |
| **Conduit** | An empty pipe in the wall with a pull-string — a path for future cables |
| **AP (access point)** | A ceiling disc that creates the Wi-Fi; one per floor |
| **PoE** | Power over Ethernet — the network cable also powers the disc, so no plug needed |
| **ONT** | The small box where the street's fibre becomes our network |
| **VLAN** | The technique that splits one set of wires into separate private networks |
| **UPS** | A battery that keeps the network alive through power blips |
| **Rough-in** | Everything installed inside the walls before plasterboard |
